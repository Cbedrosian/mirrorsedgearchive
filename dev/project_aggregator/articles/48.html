Mirror's Edge Catalyst (2016) Xbox One Review #2

When a new Mirror’s Edge game was announced all the way back at E3 2013, I, like many others, rejoiced. There was no other game I wanted to be announced as much, and after years of longing for the return of Faith, it’s hard to forget the moment. Now, in 2016, Mirror’s Edge Catalyst has finally released. It’s not perfect – no game is. But within two or three hours, Catalyst had exceeded years of expectations.

Just like the original, Mirror’s Edge Catalyst is a game that’s centred around entirely unique free-flowing parkour mechanics. While games like Assassin’s Creed have also featured parkour over the years, Catalyst delivers weighty, precise moves that feel brilliant to pull off. A large array of different moves come together to make running through Catalyst’s city of Glass an absolute pleasure. As you unlock more moves and gadgets, such as the Mag Rope, traversing the city becomes a beautiful synergy of excellently-designed gameplay mechanics.

Sections from the main missions, which see Faith desperately running away from gunfire, or towards a friend in need made me feel genuinely breathless. Frantically wallrunning, spring-boarding and ziplineing away from enemies is a thrilling experience that no game has or likely will replicate in years. That said, Mirror’s Edge Catalyst’s movement also shines in the slower moments which see the player figuring out environmental puzzles. Moments both in the open world and the missions often task you with making your own way to the top of buildings, using wallclimbs, 180 degree turns and more. These slower environmental platforming sections are often more complex and compelling than those seen in the likes of Uncharted or Tomb Raider. Whether you’re exploring the open world at your own pace or sprinting as fast as you can through missions, the world is beautifully designed for its parkour mechanics.

Unlike the original game’s sometimes jarring combat, Mirror’s Edge Catalyst features battles that are heavily ingrained with the parkour system. By ensuring that attacks such as kicks do most damage when you combine them with moves such as wallruns, Catalyst encourages you to keep on the move and to use the environment to defeat your enemies.

While Faith’s moves feel satisfyingly powerful, you’re not going to have as much fun with combat if you stand still and spam the punch or kick button. Instead, embracing the idea that Faith is a projectile that hits once and keeps on running results in a brilliantly stylish combat system. It’s rare that a game’s combat can put a smile on my face, but Mirror’s Edge Catalyst did so many times. It’s often hard not to feel like an utter badass.

While the original Mirror’s Edge had a strong narrative premise, a lack of dialogue meant that the game’s story was wafer-thin. And so I wasn’t expecting Catalyst’s story to be a masterpiece, though I did have hopes that the themes of the original would be expanded upon in an interesting way. What I didn’t realise was quite how much better Catalyst’s story and characters would be.

It’s leagues ahead of what the first game accomplished in this regard, evidenced by that I found myself eager to keep playing for the story alone. The first Mirror’s Edge never did this for me, and it’s quite rare that any action game does. Catalyst’s narrative brings up plenty of intriguing discussion points that left me wondering throughout, even if they are typical to the genre. Despite the game’s climax being emotionally charged and well-executed, it does feel a little abrupt. That said, the narrative leaves me excited to see where the series is headed.

Seeing this very solid narrative through are the characters – many of which are well-acted and characterised. Protagonist Faith and hacker Plastic’s relationship was a joy to watch evolve, for instance. The main cast are acted well enough to portray plenty of character subtleties, and it’s genuinely heart-wrenching to see some characters’ reactions to various plot-events.

It’s also worth mentioning that the game may feature one of the best fictional representations of someone that happens to be on the autistic spectrum that I’ve seen. While it’s not outright said, I believe Plastic may be autistic. As someone on the spectrum myself, I could relate to her in a lot of ways without the condition being a defining characteristic at all.

After hours spent with these characters, it’s clear that they are written well enough to be convincingly human. Surprisingly so for an action adventure game. Plenty of gorgeous cinematics also highlight character details, capturing a variety of emotions through some great cinematography and lighting.

Back before the game came out, hearing that Mirror’s Edge Catalyst would be open world both excited me and made me a little nervous. The idea of making my own way to an objective sounded fantastic, but I’m used to open worlds being not as tightly designed as linear ones – perhaps because of the sheer size.

And yet, Catalyst manages to present both a sprawling open world full of platforming options, but also environments that are incredibly well-designed. The level designers at DICE know exactly which sort of environments Faith’s traversal skillset shines in, making this likely the most well-designed open world I’ve had the pleasure of exploring.

Through five open-world areas diverse in both visuals and layout, Mirror’s Edge Catalyst’s excellent art-style is easy to spot. And that’s not even mentioning the other beautiful environments that the main missions present. Sharp, bright colours make the game frequently stunning from a visual perspective. You know it’s a good looking game when you long for a photo-mode. Unfortunately, texture pop-in and rendering issues do show up more often than I’d like, at least on the Xbox One version. Thankfully, this is not enough to take away from the overall wonderful presentation.

Returning from the original, Solar Fields delivers an excellently unique soundtrack for Catalyst that not only establishes the world through distinct sounds – but also encourages you to run and explore. Rarely does a game achieve such a synergy between music and gameplay.

Mirror’s Edge Catalyst is ultimately an incredibly well-designed game that takes the original’s vision and realises it in almost every way. Throughout the game, I frequently found myself stopping and looking out across vistas, thinking ‘this is amazing’. No piece of media can be perfect – but Mirror’s Edge Catalyst is everything I wanted and more.
