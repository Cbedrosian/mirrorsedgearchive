Silvine Security Systems. Never heard of it? You've never read the comics then. Faith's mother worked for Silvine Security Systems behind the back of Faith's father, who worked to get rid of the surveillance being made by SSS. They don't get mentioned in the first game but they are mentioned a lot in the comics released around that time. This means a prequel/reboot/whatever you want to call it of the original Mirror's Edge should have it in, right?

You're god damn right it does.

When I first started looking at the colours I didn't know of Silvine, because I'd never read the comics. In my post I kept referring to them as "Slure" or "Slune" because it was extremely difficult to work out what the word says - at first glance the logo looks like Slure/Slune, but it in fact a very stylised font.

First image. Check this out. This is a billboard you see in the distance in the gameplay trailer, right at the start. I'll tell you why it's interesting - note the type of colours used. Bright orange. Whites. Greys. When I first saw it, I thought of the animated intro slate to Mirror's Edge (original) with the white/grey floor becoming painted red in a footprint when Faith runs over it.

But the most important thing is the way it is coloured. It's paint blobs. Nothing precise, just a blob of paint, seemingly random grey lines, and some offshoots of paint around the outside. I'll tell you something else - when do you ever see blobs of paint in the original game? When do you ever see buildings where the colours aren't flat? Squares, rectangles. One or two colours per building. Whites, blues, oranges, etc. It's a heavily scrutinised city. The police are everywhere, watching, on security cameras. Why would it have any sign of creativity when no-one is allowed creativity?

So why, then, does a security camera manufacturing company have billboards with uneven, seemingly random blobs of paint? I believe Catalyst is set just before the entire city of Glass becomes completely and utterly watched. When Silvine is working on prototypes, and they're trying to gain trust. Become a family name. And then bang. Become the indisputable leaders, towering over the city of Glass.

That got me thinking. Is it a graphical bug? Did they miss it when they were editing the trailer? Or is it meant to be there, in the background, subliminal. Just like Silvine - staying in the background, getting into people's minds, and then appearing. Everyone accepts them because their brain already recognises them, thinks they're okay because they see it on their way to work, to school.

This is a pdf file of an extract from the IOSR Journal Of Humanities And Social Science, which is a double blind peer reviewed work on all sorts of stuff. I won't bore you on the details but it focuses on advertising bombardment..

Here's a snippet:

"Advertising is sometimes called mass or non personal selling. Its usual purpose is to inform, persuade and remind customers about particular products, services and ideas. It is supposed to present the advertiser's story or product as effectively and as clearly as possible, without overstepping the bounds of honesty, truthfulness and good taste. Advertising is a big business. Arens, Weigold and Arens (2008, p. 58) reveal that "it accounts for approximately 1.1 percent of the US gross domestic product (GDP)". According to them, in relation to the total US economy, this percentage is small; but it is higher than in most countries. The authors point out that it amounts to a spending level of $567.79 for every person in the country - the highest per capita in spending in the world. As Marcel Blenstein-Blanchet, the father of modern French advertising, pointed out (quoted in Arens et al 2008, p. 58) it is no coincidence that the level of advertising investment in a country is directly proportional to its standard of living.

Similarly, Packard (1957:1) observes that "Americans have become the most manipulated people, through advertising, in the world". He adds that advertising is a strange and rather exotic new area of modem life. According to him, it is about the way many people are being influenced and manipulated - far more than they realise - in the patterns of their everyday lives. Large scale efforts are being made, often with impressive success, to channel consumers "unthinking habits, their purchasing decision and their thought processes by the use of insights gleaned from psychiatry and the social sciences. Packard further says typically, these efforts take place beneath their level of awareness, so that the appeals which move them are often, in a sense, "hidden"."

You don't need to read all that, to be honest, I'll highlight the important bits.

"Its usual purpose is to inform, persuade and remind customers about particular products, services and ideas." - Inform, persuade, remind. These three things come in three stages, conveniently for me, they're in the right order already. Informing someone is you driving to work, and some radio advert time comes up, "Check out Everdyne water". You think nothing of it, it is just one of many adverts.

Then, you finish your shift of work, you're coming back home, and "Everdyne water, the most environmentally friendly water, uses 90% H2O nil yadda yadda." Persuasion.

You think to yourself, "Hey, I buy bottled water all the time for when I go out for a jog. I wonder what H2O yadda yadda is." You get your phone out, and you google what it is. "Hey, that sounds pretty cool, whatever."

Then, for the next two weeks, you drive to work and you drive past a massive sign that says "Everdyne water". This is the reminding stage. It gets more and more into the back of your mind.

After you've seen it many, many times, you go to a shop. You go to a drink aisle, and what's there? "10% off Everdyne water bottles." And you buy it. Cycle complete, now another item gets thrown in your face.

Is this what the devs are suggesting about Kruger? Silvine? Just a bit more subtle, your brain notices it, but you don't.
