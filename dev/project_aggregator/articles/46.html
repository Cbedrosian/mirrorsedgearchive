Mirror’s Edge: Catalyst – A confident step in the right direction

First-person has become the viewpoint of choice for developers trying to make something truly immersive. The action of Halo, the intensity of Rainbow Six Siege and the tense escapes of Dying Light owe a lot of their impact to keeping you locked in a close-up view of the action. But that doesn’t mean it works for every genre. You’d have a hard time enjoying a first-person FIFA soccer kickaround with a camera view that didn’t let you see your own legs.

The original Mirror’s Edge divided players as to whether it had nailed a new perspective or fallen flat on its face. Some argued that its firstperson view gave free-running an essential breath of fresh air, with each leap, landing and near-miss having more impact than ever. Others dismissed it as a frustrating mess, the restrictions of first-person making jumping a tedious exercise in guesswork. How are you supposed to judge a leap when you can’t even see your feet? If Catalyst never quite solves that problem, it’s more than made up for it with a stellar campaign and great open city that make the simple act of bounding from rooftop to rooftop a joy. After Dying Light’s baby steps, this is a walking, leaping, springboarding advert for the potential of first-person parkour in an open world environment.

Your playground is the futuristic city of Glass, where Faith Connors has just been released from Juvenile Hall. Faith immediately gets back to work as a Runner, a delivery girl who dashes from rooftop to rooftop, carrying packages and trying to avoid the mega corporations and law enforcement who generally see Runners as scum. If only they were that interesting. The lifeless cast of Catalyst tell a dull, overfamiliar tale of corporations controlling the masses, with Faith herself getting a clichéd redemption arc that’s missing certain beats (you’ll have to buy a tie-in comic to find out why she was in Juvenile Hall, as loading screens greedily informed us). Luckily it’s easy to look past a forgettable story when the parkour is this good.

Were it not for review deadlines, we would never have touched fast travel. Wall running, leaping and swinging through Glass is exhilarating, with first-person making you feel closer to the action than watching an avatar pull off similar aerobatics in Assassin’s Creed. It’s trickier too, demanding more precision and accurate timing. You’ll most likely spend your opening hour introducing Faith’s face to the pavement. Short load times and generous checkpoints kept us going at first, willing to retry tricky sequences of wallrun-jumpgrab-nope-missed-down-we-go over and over. Combinations like this eventually become second nature, without ever becoming so easy you get complacent. Rooftop layouts are varied, but consistent distances between railings and buildings make it easier to judge your leaps every time you come across them. It’s not perfect – it can still be hard to judge when exactly to make a jump in the heat of the action – and detractors of the original may moan it’s barely changed at all. We found having to learn every landing in a parkour game refreshing.

On the line

The best concession is that now Glass is open-world, there’s almost always multiple ways to tackle a run. If you’re having trouble nailing a tricky series of wallruns, try looking for another way forward. Chances are you’ll find one and it might just be quicker, too. Faith can also activate ‘Runner Vision’ which highlights a route in red. At first it feels cheaty, until you realise it’s only showing you one route to your destination, and almost never the fastest one. As your confidence grows and you learn your way around Glass, you’ll start running outside the red line more and more. It takes a lot of trial and error, but the first time you complete a delivery against the clock without even noticing a hint of red is glorious.

Runner Vision can be obtrusive whenever you’re sent indoors. The original’s interiors were a huge misstep – claustrophobic corridors that were about as free-run friendly as a sledgehammer to the spine. Catalyst rips out those cramped misjudgments for bigger indoor spaces, more focused tests of the wall running, jumping and climbing we learned outside. You can either take it slow, gradually working out the correct route, or activate Runner Vision and try for an unbroken sprint to the top. The first time we completed a building break-in through intuition alone felt fantastic, but so did the time we followed the red route through an office chase without once missing a leap. Runner Vision isn’t a gamebreaker, it just increases the speed in an action adventure that’s all about never breaking your pace.

It’s a more inspired way of helping players than the unlockable gear. The Magrope is essentially a grappling hook that lets Faith swing between certain buildings. A later upgrade latches onto grappling points and pulls her up. It flows fine with the parkour but lacks the precision that on-foot platforming demands. You’re just watching Faith being pulled up to a higher point, which lacks the satisfaction of getting up there by your own merits. One Magrope upgrade lets Faith pull down walls to make new routes and create platforms, but she can’t do this while moving. Hardly game-ruining, but we can’t help but feel that any gizmo which makes you stop running in Mirror’s Edge feels like a step backwards.

We preferred the Focus system. As long as you keep Faith moving, a Focus meter fills up. When goons shoot at Faith, her health will be protected, their hits instead draining the Focus meter. It’s an inspired addition that rewards you for running from fights. So what moron decided to defy it with unskippable fighting sections? Faith can punch and kick her way through a fight, in mediocre combat that really doesn’t need to be here. You’re supposed to use your parkour skills for more powerful hits, like running up a wall and then propelling yourself off for a nasty kick to the jaw. That’s fine during the freerunning, but a tedious slog whenever you’re trapped in an arena full of grunts. We were forced to run around in circles, Benny Hill-style, trying to get enough distance to do the leaping attacks and get this trash over with. Why not just lose the combat? It’d be A) a more fun game, and B) far easier to buy Faith as the hero if she didn’t spend her days booting law enforcement off skyscrapers.

Luckily it’s an infrequent mistake in the main campaign, with side quests relegating combat to the last resort it always should’ve been. You’ll find these extra missions all over Glass, ranging from distracting law enforcement for other Runners to delivering packages under tight time limits. They’re a smart way of helping you learn the best way to travel through the city. Whenever we failed to get a delivery on time, it made us more observant for new routes and shortcuts, or tricks we could use to shave seconds off a run. Attempt side quests regularly and you’ll soon learn what drops Faith will confidently land and which falls will send her kneecaps smashing through her jawbone.

We’re grateful for any work that keeps us sprinting through Glass for longer. The game’s shining whites and bright color palette isn’t quite as striking in 2016 as it was in the brown-and-grey wasteland that was gaming in 2008, but it’s still a striking look that resembles the Apple iCity our own doomed civilization is probably hurtling towards. Some may find that a little lacking in humanity. Bar the occasional NPC, the only people you see while running along the rooftops are being beamed out of giant screens or stuck behind thick windows, chatting to co-workers and friends, oblivious or uncaring to your presence. We can see why that might make the city feel cold, but to us it helped sell Faith’s status as an outsider. Free-running with only the sounds of your footsteps and the excellent Solar Fields score offers such immersive isolation, who needs other people anyway? It’s far longer than the original, but doesn’t outstay its welcome thanks to a campaign that finds constant clever tests of the core parkour. Varied setpieces are also strong, such as a sprint up a rooftop of shattering glass and a late game use for the Magrope which is the closest you can get to flying without wings. The best sequences might be the skyscraper escapes, where the parkour skills you’ve honed to take you to higher ground now have to be adjusted for a swift descent. They say you should handle heights by never looking down. DICE gleefully remove that option.

Then there are the moments you inevitably do miss a jump. The fall is brutal, the sound of Faith panicking and the wind roaring in your ears as the camera refuses to cut away until just before she hits the ground. Death is so common in games it’s hard to get a reaction from us anymore, but even in Catalyst’s final hours, taking the plunge could still illicit a gasp – just another benefit of locking the action in first person. Maybe Catalyst is a slightly too conservative sequel to a true original, but this open world sequel is still a great step forward.
