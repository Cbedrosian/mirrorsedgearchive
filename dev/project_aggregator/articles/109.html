Mirror's Edge: Exordium Is Your First Leap into the Rebooted Game World

Trust your instinct, close your eyes, and leap into the prequel comic.

By Joshua Yehl
Even though Mirror's Edge Catalyst doesn't come out until next February, Dark Horse Comics is giving us a taste of things to come with a prequel comic, Mirror's Edge: Exordium. We know that Catalyst is a reboot, so Exordium will be our first step into Faith's new world before the game comes out.

To start things off, we have your first look at the Books-a-Million exclusive variant cover for the first issue.

Books-a-Million exclusive variant cover for Mirror’s Edge: Exordium #1

To get the scoop on the upcoming comic, we conducted an email interview with Chistopher Emgard, the writer of both Mirror's Edge Catalyst and Exordium. We talked to him about what to expect from Exordium, how they aim to capture the free-running feel of the game in the comic, and how the comic will link to the game. Hear what Emgard had to say, then take to the comments with your thoughts.

IGN Comics: What’s the quick pitch for Mirror’s Edge: Exordium?

Christofer Emgard: Faith has grown up off the grid among the runners of Glass, but even though she is fast and daring, her mentor and adoptive father Noah is reluctant to give her real assignments. He considers her reckless and hotheaded, and the schism between them is growing daily.

Secretly defying him, Faith does runs on the side, and one day she stumbles on a rare link to her dark past. The discovery sets off a chain of events which will force her to reexamine many things about herself, and in the end she must risk everything in order to stay true to her values.

IGN: How much of Faith’s backstory will we see in the comic? How is this version of Faith different than the one we played in the 2008 game?

Emgard: The Exordium story arc narrates events spanning about a week – all of it taking place roughly two years before Mirror’s Edge Catalyst begins.

Compared to the first Mirror’s Edge, I would say that Faith in Exordium is more of a seeker, still trying to figure out how to relate to the world around her. I guess she’s a bit more insecure as well, hungry for approval and sometimes making questionable choices in order to get it. Choices that bring consequences.

It’s also worth noting, again, that we’re doing a complete reboot. Though there are themes, ideas and characters in Exordium and Mirror’s Edge Catalyst that longtime fans might recognize, there are no narrative connections to the first game and comic.

IGN: With you writing both the comic and the game, are there any elements that link the two?

Emgard: They can be experienced as stand-alone and self-contained, but Mirror’s Edge Catalyst and Exordium are deeply intertwined. Events that are set in motion in Exordium will continue to play out in Mirror’s Edge Catalyst , and a handful of characters appear in both the game and the comic. From a thematic standpoint, Faith’s journey in Exordium is one of self-discovery and personal growth, and that’s an ongoing process which stretches across both the comic and the game, and beyond.

IGN: What are artists Mattias Haggstrom and Robert Sammelin bringing to the comic that will capture the feel of the video game?

Emgard: Mattias and Robert are deeply involved in the development of the game itself and they have an intimate knowledge of the characters and the city of Glass. Mattias is the storyboard artist and Director of Photography for our cut-scenes. He’s an expert visual storyteller and from the get-go he’s done an amazing job of translating my scripts into imagery, often finding interesting angles and blocking solutions that really add to the scenes. The process has been the same for the comic, and this means that from a visual perspective there’s a clear carryover between the comic and our in-game cut-scenes.

Robert is responsible for key art and other conceptual artwork relating to the game, and he’s ensured that the stylistic elegance of the city of Glass and the characters inhabiting it has been maintained in the comic. He designed the comic version of Faith and the other characters in close collaboration with our character concept lead and I think people who play Mirror’s Edge Catalyst and read Exordium will have no trouble recognizing the city or the characters.

IGN: What would you say is the appeal of the first Mirror’s Edge game that you hope to capture in the new game and in this comic?

Emgard: First of all, I would say Faith herself and the mystery of who she is. She has extraordinary abilities but she’s no superhero, and though she can be very assertive, she also harbors many doubts and there are things she need to resolve in order to fully come into her own.

Second would be the parkour, and all the dizzying, vertigo-inducing stunts and death-defying moves that come with it. The first game conveyed that excellently and we’re pushing the movement even harder this time around, putting a lot of work into fluidity and controls. For the comic there will be also plenty of running, but it has also been an opportunity to show more interaction between the characters, and to delve a little deeper into Faith’s mind.

Last but not least, the overarching themes of surveillance and control. What’s the price of freedom? How much privacy are we willing to give up in order to feel safe? And what if we one day discover that all of it has gone too far? These questions seem to resonate with a lot of people, and they’re very relevant to both the world we’re building for the game and comic.

IGN: The first Mirror’s Edge had a theme of government control versus personal freedom. Will that be the theme of this comic and the new game, or will new avenues be explored?

Emgard: We have replaced “government” with “corporate”, but yes, that theme still permeates the world of both Mirror’s Edge Catalyst and Exordium. We are also exploring the populace’s complicity in their own oppression, and the fact that many look away while others suffer, but these things are more about expanding the theme than changing it.

With that said, the story is mainly focused on Faith and her journey. She does become involved in the struggle for overarching change, but she’s driven by very personal motives and these are really what propels the story forward in both the comic and the game.

IGN: What would you say to fans of Mirror’s Edge to get them excited for this comic?

Emgard: The comic sets up one of the most important relationships Faith has, and think anyone wanting to know more about who she is and what drives her will want to have a look at Exordium.
